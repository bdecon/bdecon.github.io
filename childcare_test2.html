Childcare as Infrastructure

The US came within reach of public childcare. Twice.

"These children are future citizens, and if they are neglected in these early years it will hurt not only the children themselves, but the community as a whole. Where state help is needed, it should be given; and when states are incapable of giving sufficient help, it should be forthcoming on a national scale." Eleanor Roosevelt, September 8, 1945, as WWII childcare centers were closing across the country

During World War II, the federal government built public childcare centers so that mothers could work in shipyards and factories. It was, as the economist Claudia Goldin noted, "a national, practically universal, federally funded preschool program. It is, to this day, the only one." When the war ended, the centers closed.

Federally funded childcare center.
New Britain, CT, 1943. Photo: Gordon Parks

In 1971, Congress passed the Comprehensive Child Development Act, a bipartisan bill to establish universal childcare. President Nixon vetoed it. His speechwriter, Pat Buchanan, drafted a message warning against "the vast moral authority of the National Government" being committed "to the side of communal approaches to child rearing over against the family-centered approach."

Not all countries failed

The Nordic countries — Denmark, Finland, Norway, and Sweden — built their systems through political contest, not consensus. In 1934, Swedish social scientists Alva and Gunnar Myrdal argued for sharing the responsibility of child-rearing between families and society. It took forty years of organizing — by labor unions, by women's movements, by social democratic parties — before Sweden passed its National Preschool Act in 1975. Finland guaranteed every child a legal right to public care from birth. Denmark and Norway followed. Those systems survived because they worked, and because the people who depended on them organized to protect them.

The four Nordic countries all have high-quality, affordable childcare, and all follow the same basic model: public, universal childcare funded through taxes. The details vary. The overall results do not. The Nordics charge families little or nothing, enroll far more children, pay childcare workers a livable wage, and run their systems through the public sector. Meanwhile, the United States ranks 40th out of 41 wealthy countries on childcare, according to UNICEF's most recent assessment. This report asks why the US looks so different.

A subnational view

A common response to comparisons like these is that the Nordic countries are small, and that wealthy US states like Massachusetts already perform at Nordic levels. In the case of childcare, they do not. Even the richest US states fall far short on public childcare, and a vicious cycle of underfunding makes the gap self-reinforcing, though places like New York City and New Mexico are trying to break out of it.

The first section compares childcare systems between the US and Nordic countries, find a large gap in funding, enrollment, wages, and employment. Where possible, analysis is extended to US states, New York City, and the Canadian provinces. The subnational view confirms that the gaps persist at the state level in every state. The final section explains downstream consequences of the large gap in childcare infrastructure, as well as recent attempts by states and municipalities to reduce the gap.

The money is there. The system is not.

The most common defense of the US childcare gap is that the country can't afford to do what the Nordics do. This section eliminates that explanation. The US has comparable resources per child. It has a comparable share of young children in its population. The gap between the US and Nordic childcare systems is not a gap in resources. It is a gap in what those resources are spent on.

The money is there

The US is not too poor to fund childcare. It is richer per child than every Nordic country except Norway. Every place discussed in this report is rich by global standards. One way to see this is Gross Domestic Product per young child — the annual economic activity of an area divided by its number of young children. GDP per young child serves as a rough measure of the financial resources available in a society for each child it needs to support. The global average is about $190,000 per child aged 0-5. The US generates $1.28 million. Denmark generates $1.23 million. Finland generates $1.05 million. Massachusetts, at $2.41 million, has triple the per child resources of Sweden.

The differences in material resources between the US, Canada, and Nordic countries are relatively small. A much bigger gap exists between wealthy societies and the rest of the world. Likewise, a bigger gap exists within each of these places, in how resources are distributed. The important takeaway is that national and state-level resources per child are abundant. Nordic countries on the lower end of the GDP per young child measure have built high-quality public childcare systems without creating financial hardship.

And the need for childcare is there

Nor is the gap explained by demographics. The US has roughly the same share of young children as the Nordics — between 5% and 7% of the total population (chart 1B). The difference is what happens to them before age 5.

Young children in every country need full-time supervision — someone feeds them, watches them, teaches them, every day, for years. Whether the public sector offers childcare or not, the same childcare needs exist.

In the US, the government pays for education from kindergarten through high school — thirteen years of free public school. Before kindergarten, families pay for expensive childcare out of pocket, or a parent leaves work, or a grandparent fills in. Some public programs cover part of the gap for some children, but most families are on their own. Other wealthy countries solved childcare the same way the US solved K-12: by viewing it as a universal public service.

How Sweden prices childcare

In Sweden, parents drop their children off at a public daycare in their neighborhood and pay a fee based on their income — 3% for the first child, 2% for the second, 1% for the third, and nothing after that. The fee is capped, so no family pays more than about $2,100 a year per child regardless of income.

In the US, a family earning $40,000 pays the same price as a family earning $400,000.

But US public childcare funding is not there

If resources are abundant and the need is the same, the gap must come from funding decisions. It does. The US spends 0.3% of GDP on public childcare and early education for children under 5 — about 50 cents per American per day. The Nordic countries spend around 1.3% of GDP. The entire gap between last-place US childcare and top-ranked Nordic systems boils down to one percent of GDP.

When compared to Nordic countries, the US does have a weaker public sector overall. But the childcare funding gap is disproportionate even by that standard. US public funding for childcare is simply not there.

So the services and infrastructure do not exist

Public funding creates public slots. Public slots create enrollment. This is not complicated. Countries that fund childcare have near-universal enrollment. The US, which funds almost nothing, enrolls almost no one.

Nordic countries enroll children starting at age 1, when parental leave ends. By age 3, enrollment exceeds 80% in every Nordic country. The United States public care for children begins in earnest at age 5 with kindergarten. Before that, some states offer preschool for age 4, with mixed results. A few areas in the US are trying to push further to cover younger children. Otherwise, US families are mostly on their own. In effect, the US has no public childcare system for children under 3. At age 2, the US enrollment rate in public programs is effectively 0%.

Low enrollment is not a choice families are making. It reflects a physical absence. Even if Congress appropriated the money tomorrow, the buildings, the trained workers, and the licensed slots to serve every family do not exist. The US has no childcare infrastructure for most children, in the same literal sense that a city with no airport has no aviation infrastructure.

Buchanan was afraid the program would work — that families would use it, depend on it, and defend it at the ballot box. That is exactly what happened in every country that built one.

No US state — not Massachusetts, not Minnesota, not California — has built childcare infrastructure comparable to any Nordic country. A system designed to fail

The previous section established that the US childcare gap is not caused by lack of resources, demographics, or state-level variation. It is a funding decision, and that decision has consequences. This section traces what happens downstream: which choices families actually face, who leaves the workforce, who falls into poverty, and why the system resists repair.

First, a comparison of the experiences of a parent of a young child in the US and in Finland to see which system offers more freedom.

A tale of two systems

You have a child under 5. If you live in the United States, you choose between private childcare at about $15,000 per year or one parent leaves the workforce. If you live in Finland, you choose between public childcare at $0-4,080 per year or a home care allowance of ~$4,500 per year paid to the parent. Same situation. Radically different options.

The illustration above is not an abstract comparison. Finland grants every child a legal right to public early childhood education and care from birth. The home care allowance is an option layered on top of a complete public system. Both options are funded. Both options are real.

In any society, some parents will prefer to care for their children at home. Finland gives them freedom to pursue that option — and lets them move between home care and public daycare as needs change. Staying home to care for a young child means lost income, so the allowance fills the gap, preventing poverty.

In the United States, Head Start offers part-day preschool to some low-income children. Scattered state pre-K programs cover some four-year-olds for a few hours during the school year. For most families, the only option for obtaining outside childcare services is the private market.

Results described in the second section of the report show that the apparent freedom of the private market is a constraint in disguise. Families pay outrageous rates, if they even can, piece together informal arrangements, or someone — usually the mother — leaves paid work. That is not choice. It is the daily emergency resulting from the national removal of viable choices.

The cost to families

3.8% of US working-age adults are out of the labor force for childcare. In the Nordic countries, the figure is under 1%. Nine in ten of these sidelined adults are women. Outside of recessions, more Americans are out of work for childcare than for unemployment.

The economics are straightforward. Full-time childcare costs about $15,000 per child. At a household income of $200,000, that is a line item. At $40,000, it is more than a third of everything the family makes before taxes. For a family with two children, that is $30,000 a year—close to what a worker at the bottom third of the pay scale takes home. For many families, paying for care costs more than the second earner brings home. Without a public option, someone stays home, and overwhelmingly that someone is the mother.

The poverty consequences of the childcare gap are severe for the US. Among mothers of young children who left work for caregiving, one in four lives below the poverty line. Among those who stayed employed full-time, the rate is under 6%. For single mothers of young children, the poverty rate reaches 31%. The pattern runs in one direction: no infrastructure forces women out of the workforce, and workforce exit pushes families into poverty.

The cost to employers

The childcare gap does not only push families into poverty. It drains the businesses trying to hire them.

When a parent leaves the workforce because care is unavailable, their employer loses a trained worker. When a parent cuts hours or calls in because a care arrangement fell through, their employer pays for it in turnover and lost output. No company's budget has a line for "childcare." It shows up as unfilled positions and retention problems — especially in healthcare, manufacturing, and food service, where schedules are inflexible and the workforce skews female.

Nordic employers do not offer childcare stipends or run backup care programs. They pay taxes and get a full labor force in return. The public system solves the problem at its source. This is not charity. It is infrastructure — the same way a public road network is more efficient than asking every business to pave its own path to the highway.

The typical childcare worker makes $15.41 per hour, or $32,050 a year. 99% of occupations pay more. An elementary school teacher at the school next door makes $62,340. That gap is not a market outcome. It is a policy choice about which work counts as professional.

An elementary school teacher's salary is not particularly generous, either. Neither wage reflects the value of the work. But there is a practical case for closing the gap: professional pay attracts trained workers, training improves quality, and the returns from quality early care flow disproportionately to the children who need it most.

Without professional pay, here is the competition for childcare labor: the lowest-paid occupation in the US, a fast-food cook, makes only 6% less per hour. A dogwalker makes more. Why the market can't fix it

The childcare enrollment and poverty numbers above are not random. They are the product of two feedback loops, one in each direction.

In Nordic countries, public investment creates a professional workforce, which produces quality care, which builds public trust, which sustains the investment. Even Finland's current right-wing government has not touched childcare. Conservative, liberal, and socialist parents send their kids to the same daycares. Finland's childcare system is politically untouchable because it works and people depend on it.

In the United States, the feedback runs the other direction. Parents pay a lot. Workers are paid little. Both sides of the market are broken at the same time. Underfunding produces low wages, which cause turnover, which erodes quality, which erodes trust, which becomes the argument against investment. The Bipartisan Policy Center estimates the US loses $216-329 billion annually from inadequate childcare: lost earnings, lost productivity, lost tax revenue. Inaction is not neutral. It actively destroys value.

Expanding a patchy, income-restricted, mostly-private, childcare system is not likely to produce a better result. Research by Brown and Herbst shows that when minimum wages rise, childcare prices increase 4-8% for every 10% wage increase, because labor is 70% of costs and profit margins are under 1%. At restaurants, the same wage increase raises prices 0.4%. You cannot raise childcare worker wages without making care unaffordable for parents — unless the government structures costs differently, the way the Nordics do.

This is the structural trap. In a normal market, you can raise wages by raising prices. In childcare, you cannot — because the consumers are families who are already stretched to their limit, and there is no public payer to absorb the difference. Every other country that solved this problem solved it the same way: the government became the primary funder, set professional wages, and charged families a fraction of the cost. There is no private-market path from $15 an hour to a professional wage without making childcare unaffordable. The only question is whether the public sector steps in.

The last time the US tried to break the cycle, the design of the fix revealed the problem. In 2021, the Build Back Better Act proposed some important improvements to the childcare system, including wage standards. But the childcare subsidy was limited to lower-income families, so families just above the cutoff faced a $13,000 price increase. This inefficient design stems from an unwillingness to offer universal programs, even when they lower costs. Universal public services build the kind of broad constituency that becomes difficult to dismantle. Income-restricted public childcare systems have a trust-eroding design flaw built into them.

Breaking the cycle: a full system and a few early attempts

Building a childcare system requires solving several problems at once: funding, physical infrastructure, workforce, and political durability. Finland solved all of them over a century. The US examples below have each broken one link in the chain. None has yet assembled the full system.

Finland shows what a complete system looks like. Public childcare runs from the end of parental leave until school begins. The alternative is a home care allowance of roughly $4,500 a year, paid directly to the parent. Both options are supported by the state. The system was built over a century: first kindergarten in 1888, the Day Care Act in 1973, university-educated staff required by 1995. Staff ratios are one adult per four children, the lowest in the OECD.

New York City is solving the infrastructure problem — building the physical system age by age. Pre-K for All made public preschool available to every four-year-old starting in 2014. 3-K extended that to three-year-olds. In January 2026, the city launched a Birth-to-2 pilot offering free full-day care for infants and toddlers at 15 sites across the city, with no income requirements. A week later, Mayor Mamdani and Governor Hochul announced 2-Care, a plan to make care for all two-year-olds free by the end of the decade, with $500 million committed for the first two years.

The ambition is real. So is the problem underneath it: home-based childcare providers have collapsed from 28,000 to 12,000 since 2007, and childcare workers are still paid about $36,500 a year in a city where public school teachers make nearly triple that.

New Mexico solved the funding problem — writing permanent revenue into the constitution. In 2022, voters approved a constitutional amendment directing oil royalties from the Land Grant Permanent Fund into early childhood education. In 2025, New Mexico became the first US state to offer universal childcare, open to any family regardless of income. By writing the funding into the constitution, the state insulated it from the annual budget fights that have stalled progress everywhere else. The money is structurally protected. The workforce is not. Whether it produces quality depends on fights that have not happened yet.

Vermont solved the political problem — building a coalition broad enough to override a veto. In 2023, the legislature passed Act 76, creating a dedicated 0.44% payroll tax that generates roughly $125 million a year for childcare. The governor vetoed it. The legislature overrode the veto. Subsidy eligibility now reaches families earning up to $175,000, and copays are eliminated for families below $55,000.

Behind the legislation was a decade of sustained advocacy: a philanthropic organization called Let's Grow Kids raised $70 million, built a statewide coalition including business owners and manufacturers, and then deliberately shut itself down once the state took ownership. For the first time since 2018, more childcare programs are opening in Vermont than closing.

Closing the gap

We can build it

The United States has roughly the same GDP per young child as Nordic countries — abundant resources for childcare. The US also has the existing model for public care — thirteen years of free public education, with 90% enrollment. What the US does not have is infrastructure or public funding for kids during their first five years. The US spends almost nothing on public childcare for ages 0 to 4. Importantly, young kids are a small slice of each population, so the high-coverage childcare systems in the Nordic countries cost only 1% of GDP above what the public spends now.

The cost of acting is not particularly high, but the cost of inaction has been very high. More Americans are out of work for childcare than for unemployment. Childcare workers are paid less than 99% of all occupations, including dogwalkers. Nearly 1 in every 5 US children live in poverty. These are the outcomes of political choices. The current system cannot produce a different outcome.

The case studies above show that the choice can go differently. Finland built a complete system over a century. New York City is building one age by age. New Mexico wrote the funding into its constitution. Vermont passed a payroll tax over a governor's veto. None of these happened easily. All of them happened because people organized and fought for them.

"It's not a matter of my children and your children, but our children — all children." Olof Palme, Prime Minister of Sweden

Supporting a system that covers all children is a political argument, not a sentimental one. The Nordic systems were not inevitable. They were built by labor movements and working-class organizers who fought for them. They are the effect of decades of refinement. Nordic childcare systems are politically untouchable today because they work, and no party that wants to win an election will take them away.

The gap between the US and the Nordic countries is wide. It was created by political choices and maintained by political interests. But it is not locked in place. Once a high-quality universal preschool and childcare example is built in the US, it will serve workers, employers, children, and women simultaneously — it will survive and it will spread.
