Childcare as Infrastructure

Brian Dew

The US came within reach of public childcare. Twice.

"These children are future citizens, and if they are neglected in these early years it will hurt not only the children themselves, but the community as a whole. Where state help is needed, it should be given; and when states are incapable of giving sufficient help, it should be forthcoming on a national scale." Eleanor Roosevelt, September 8, 1945, as WWII childcare centers were closing across the country

During World War II, the federal government built public childcare centers so that mothers could work in shipyards and factories. It was, as the economist Claudia Goldin noted, "a national, practically universal, federally funded preschool program. It is, to this day, the only one." When the war ended, the centers closed.

Federally funded childcare center, New Britain, Connecticut, 1943.
Photo: Gordon Parks / Library of Congress

In 1971, Congress passed the Comprehensive Child Development Act, a bipartisan bill to establish universal childcare. President Nixon vetoed it. His speechwriter, Pat Buchanan, drafted a message warning against "the vast moral authority of the National Government" being committed "to the side of communal approaches to child rearing over against the family-centered approach."

The Nordic countries — Denmark, Finland, Norway, and Sweden — built their systems through political contest, not consensus. In 1934, Swedish social scientists Alva and Gunnar Myrdal argued for sharing the responsibility of child-rearing between families and society. It took forty years of organizing — by labor unions, by women's movements, by social democratic parties — before Sweden passed its National Preschool Act in 1975. Finland guaranteed every child a legal right to public care from birth. Denmark and Norway followed. Those systems survived because they worked, and because the people who depended on them organized to protect them.

The four Nordic countries all have high-quality, affordable childcare, and all follow the same basic model: public, universal childcare funded through taxes. The details vary. The overall results do not. The Nordics charge families little or nothing, enroll far more children, pay childcare workers a livable wage, and run their systems through the public sector. Meanwhile, the United States ranks 40th out of 41 wealthy countries on childcare, according to UNICEF's most recent assessment. This report asks why the US looks so different.

A common response to comparisons like these is that the Nordic countries are small, and that wealthy US states like Massachusetts already perform at Nordic levels. In the case of childcare, they do not. Even the richest US states fall far short on public childcare, and a vicious cycle of underfunding makes the gap self-reinforcing, though places like New York City and New Mexico are trying to break out of it.

This report compares childcare systems across US states, New York City, the Canadian provinces, and the Nordic countries — looking at spending, enrollment, wages, and costs to show the gap between North American and Nordic systems.

What some countries built

The money is there

Every country on this page is extraordinarily rich by any global standard. One way to see this is GDP per child — the total economic output of a country divided by its number of young children. It is a rough measure of how much a society produces for each child it needs to support. The global average is about $190,000 per child aged 0-5. The US generates $1.28 million. Denmark generates $1.23 million. Finland generates $1.05 million. Massachusetts, at $2.41 million, produces more than double Finland per child.

The difference is not in wealth but in how it is distributed. Paychecks go to working-age adults with jobs. That is true everywhere. The question is what happens next. The Nordics tax that income broadly and redirect it toward people who are not earning — young children, the parents caring for them, the elderly, the disabled. The US, for childcare, does almost none of this. Families bear the full cost at the moment they can least afford it.

When children reach kindergarten, the US government covers the full cost of their education — for thirteen years. Before kindergarten, families pay for childcare out of pocket, at prices that can consume a large share of a working family's income. Some public programs fill part of the gap for some children, but most families are on their own. The spending gap between early childhood and K-12 is not gradual. It is a cliff.

Råå Day Care Center, Helsingborg, Sweden. Architect: Dorte Mandrup. Photo: Adam Mørk

In Sweden, parents drop their children off at a public daycare in their neighborhood and pay a fee based on their income — 3% for the first child, 2% for the second, 1% for the third, and nothing after that. The fee is capped, so no family pays more than about $2,100 a year per child regardless of income. An American family pays the same price whether they make $40,000 or $400,000.

A gap in priorities

The US spends less than 0.3% of GDP on early childhood education and care — about 50 cents per American per day. GDP is a measure of a country's total economic output, so this percentage shows how much of its resources a country directs toward childcare. The Nordic countries spend over 1.3%. The US does have a smaller government overall — it collects and spends about 39% of GDP, compared to 47-56% in the Nordics. But the childcare gap is disproportionate even by that standard. It is not just less government. It is a specific absence.

Who gets in

Spending becomes enrollment. Where countries fund childcare systems, children show up. Eight European countries guarantee a childcare slot from age 6-18 months. Sweden caps parent fees at 3% of income. These are commitments written into law. The enrollment numbers reflect it.

Nordic countries enroll children starting at age 1, when parental leave ends. By age 3, enrollment exceeds 80% in every Nordic country. The United States has no public childcare for children under 3. At age 2, the US enrollment rate in public programs is 0%.

The infrastructure gap

The result is not just a spending gap or an enrollment gap. It is a physical gap. Fewer trained workers, fewer licensed facilities, fewer available slots. The financial resources exist in the abstract. The concrete system — the buildings, the people, the laws — does not. Both countries know how to build world-class infrastructure. Only one builds childcare.

The empty lot is not an accident. Pat Buchanan later reflected on killing the 1971 childcare bill:

"If we hadn't stopped it, I think you'd have an entitlement program now of enormous size with these federal day care centers, and they would be growing and growing and growing once you got it on the books." Patrick Buchanan, NPR interview

Buchanan was afraid the program would work — that families would use it, depend on it, and defend it at the ballot box. That is exactly what happened in every country that built one.

No US state has built comparable childcare infrastructure either. Nordic countries are small — but so are US states. Massachusetts has a larger economy than Finland. That is precisely why this next section compares at the state level: it removes the excuse that the US is simply too big or too diverse for the comparison to hold. What the gap means

A tale of two systems

You have a child under 5. If you live in the United States, you choose between private childcare at about $15,000 per year or one parent leaves the workforce. If you live in Finland, you choose between public childcare at $0-4,080 per year or a home care allowance of ~$4,500 per year paid to the parent. Same situation. Radically different options.

This is not an abstract comparison. Finland grants every child a legal right to public early childhood education and care from birth. The home care allowance is an option layered on top of a complete public system. Both options are funded. Both options are real.

In any society, some parents will prefer to care for their children at home. Finland gives them that option — and lets them move between home care and public daycare as needs change. Staying home means lost income, so the allowance fills the gap.

In the United States, Head Start offers part-day preschool to some low-income children. Scattered state pre-K programs cover some four-year-olds for a few hours during the school year. For most families, the only real choice is the private market.

The cost of doing nothing

3.8% of US working-age adults are out of the labor force for childcare. In the Nordic countries, the figure is under 1%. Nine in ten of these sidelined adults are women. Outside of recessions, more Americans are out of work for childcare than for unemployment.

The economics are straightforward. Full-time childcare costs about $15,000 per child. At a household income of $200,000, that is a line item. At $40,000, it is more than a third of everything the family makes before taxes. For a family with two children, that is $30,000 a year—close to what a worker at the bottom third of the pay scale takes home. For many families, paying for care costs more than the second earner brings home. When there is no public option, someone stays home, and overwhelmingly that someone is the mother.

The poverty consequences are severe. Among mothers of young children who left work for caregiving, one in four lives below the poverty line. Among those who stayed employed full-time, the rate is under 6%. For single mothers of young children, the poverty rate reaches 31%. The pattern runs in one direction: no infrastructure forces women out of the workforce, and workforce exit pushes families into poverty.

The feedback loop

These outcomes are not random. They are the product of two feedback loops, one in each direction.

In Nordic countries, public investment creates a professional workforce, which produces quality care, which builds public trust, which sustains the investment. Even Finland's current right-wing government has not touched childcare. Conservative, liberal, and socialist parents send their kids to the same daycares. The system is politically untouchable because it works and people depend on it.

In the United States, the feedback runs the other direction. Parents pay a lot. Workers earn little. Both sides of the market are broken at the same time. Underfunding produces low wages, which cause turnover, which erodes quality, which erodes trust, which becomes the argument against investment. The Bipartisan Policy Center estimates the US loses $216-329 billion annually from inadequate childcare: lost earnings, lost productivity, lost tax revenue. Inaction is not neutral. It actively destroys value.

The last time the US tried to break the cycle, the design of the fix revealed the problem. In 2021, the Build Back Better Act proposed raising childcare worker pay to match elementary school teachers — roughly $60,000. But the subsidy was limited to lower-income families, so families just above the cutoff faced a $13,000 price increase. The US can price the solution. What it will not do is fund it universally, because universal public services build the kind of broad constituency that becomes difficult to dismantle.

The current rules cannot produce a different result. Research by Brown and Herbst shows that when minimum wages rise, childcare prices increase 4-8% for every 10% wage increase, because labor is 70% of costs and profit margins are under 1%. At restaurants, the same wage increase raises prices 0.4%. You cannot raise childcare worker wages without making care unaffordable for parents — unless the government structures costs differently, the way the Nordics do.

US childcare workers are paid $15.41 per hour

The median childcare worker is paid $15.41 per hour, or about $32,050 a year. 99% of all occupations pay more. An elementary school teacher at the school next door is paid $62,340. That gap is not a market outcome. It is a policy choice about which work counts as professional.

The elementary school teacher's salary is not generous. States cut real teacher pay aggressively enough through the late 2010s to provoke the largest wave of teacher strikes in a generation. That depressed benchmark is still nearly double what a childcare worker takes home. Neither wage reflects the value of the work. But there is a practical case for closing the gap: professional pay attracts trained workers, training improves quality, and the returns from quality early care flow disproportionately to the children who need it most.

The peer list changes by location. In some states, childcare workers are paid a poverty wage—half of what a typical worker makes. In every Nordic country, they are paid 65–75% of a typical wage.

Breaking the cycle

The federal government has not acted since 1971. So cities and states are building on their own. None have replicated what Finland built. But the attempts reveal what it takes.

Finland shows what a complete system looks like. Public childcare runs from the end of parental leave until school begins. The alternative is a home care allowance of roughly $4,500 a year, paid directly to the parent. Both options are supported by the state. The system was built over a century: first kindergarten in 1888, the Day Care Act in 1973, university-educated staff required by 1995. Staff ratios are one adult per four children, the lowest in the OECD.

New York City is building age by age. Pre-K for All made public preschool available to every four-year-old starting in 2014. 3-K extended that to three-year-olds. In January 2026, the city launched a Birth-to-2 pilot offering free full-day care for infants and toddlers at 15 sites across the city, with no income requirements. A week later, Mayor Mamdani and Governor Hochul announced 2-Care, a plan to make care for all two-year-olds free by the end of the decade, with $500 million committed for the first two years. The ambition is real. So is the problem underneath it: home-based childcare providers have collapsed from 28,000 to 12,000 since 2007, and childcare workers are still paid about $36,500 a year in a city where public school teachers make more than double that.

New Mexico changed the funding structure itself. In 2022, voters approved a constitutional amendment directing oil royalties from the Land Grant Permanent Fund into early childhood education. In 2025, New Mexico became the first US state to offer universal childcare, open to any family regardless of income. By writing the funding into the constitution, the state insulated it from the annual budget fights that have stalled progress everywhere else. The money is structurally protected. The workforce is not. Whether it produces quality depends on fights that have not happened yet.

Vermont found a replicable funding model. In 2023, the legislature passed Act 76, creating a dedicated 0.44% payroll tax that generates roughly $125 million a year for childcare. The governor vetoed it. The legislature overrode the veto. Subsidy eligibility now reaches families earning up to $175,000, and copays are eliminated for families below $55,000. Behind the legislation was a decade of sustained advocacy: a philanthropic organization called Let's Grow Kids raised $70 million, built a statewide coalition including business owners and manufacturers, and then deliberately shut itself down once the state took ownership. For the first time since 2018, more childcare programs are opening in Vermont than closing.

Universal public childcare programs, selected cases

The United States has the resources — more GDP per young child than any Nordic country. It has the model — thirteen years of free public education, with 90% enrollment. What it does not have is the first five years. The government spends less than 0.3% of GDP on childcare, compared to over 1.3% in the Nordics. More Americans are out of work for childcare than for unemployment. Childcare workers are paid less than 99% of all occupations. The gap is not a matter of wealth. It is a matter of political choice.

The case studies above show that the choice can go differently. Finland built a complete system over a century. New York City is building one age by age. New Mexico wrote the funding into its constitution. Vermont passed a payroll tax over a governor's veto. None of these happened easily. All of them happened because people organized and fought for them.

"It's not a matter of my children and your children, but our children — all children." Olof Palme, Prime Minister of Sweden

That is a political argument, not a sentimental one. The Nordic systems were not inevitable. They were built by labor movements and working-class organizers who fought for them against real opposition, within living memory. Finland's system is politically untouchable today because three generations of families have used it, and no party that wants to win an election will take it away.

The gap between the US and the Nordic countries is wide and growing. Political choices created that gap, and political interests maintain the status quo. Once built, public childcare serves workers, employers, children, and women simultaneously — which is why these systems have survived decades of government turnover across the political spectrum. Building a comparable system in the US means building a universal one — programs that serve everyone are programs that survive.
