In the US, about 7.3 million working-age adults (3.8 percent of the total) are out of the labor force because of childcare. Nine in ten of those adults are women, mostly mothers. In Denmark, Finland, Norway, and Sweden, the share is under 1 percent.

The four Nordic countries provide paid parental leave and publicly funded childcare. In the US, families cover the cost themselves, or one parent leaves work.

## Where the gap comes from

The OECD breakdown for mothers with a child aged 0 to 2:

| Country | At work | On parental or maternity leave | Total employed | Not employed |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Denmark | 58.9% | 16.3% | 75.2% | 24.8% |
| Sweden | 50.0% | 24.7% | 74.7% | 25.3% |
| Finland | 43.2% | 18.5% | 61.7% | 38.3% |
| United States | 57.5% | 2.9% | 60.5% | 39.5% |

Source: [OECD Family Database, LMF1.2](https://webfs.oecd.org/els-com/Family_Database/LMF1_2_Maternal_Employment.pdf), latest year per country (2020 or 2021).

US mothers of infants are at work at essentially the same rate as Danish mothers (57.5 vs 58.9 percent). The Denmark-US gap in total employment comes from the on-leave column: 16 percent of Danish infant mothers are on paid leave at any given moment, against 3 percent of US mothers. Same survey rule on both sides. The difference is how much paid leave actually exists.

The share out for family care runs from 29 percent at the youngest end down to 10 percent for mothers with teens, and from 24 percent at family income under $67,000 down to 11 percent above $125,000. Roughly half of the 7.3 million are in families earning under $67,000. One in three of these mothers is in poverty (40 percent for single mothers).

The math behind the choice: US childcare runs about $15,000 a year per kid. For families in the bottom income third, two kids in care can eat half of pre-tax income. The cost is weighed against the lower earner's take-home, usually the mother's. When the math fails, she leaves.

## What Finland does

A public childcare slot at $0 to $4,080 a year, or a $5,000 home care allowance for the parent who stays home. The allowance sits on top of a complete public childcare system, so a Finnish parent picking the stay-home path is choosing between two genuine options. The US bundle lacks both pieces.
