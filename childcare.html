Childcare as Infrastructure

How the US, Canada, and Nordic countries organize early care

In Nordic countries, childcare is treated as economic infrastructure. In the United States, it is treated as a private household problem. Canada is somewhere in between, with a new national system still taking shape. This page compares how these three approaches play out across spending, wages, and enrollment.

Government spending is where the gap is most dramatic. Nordic countries spend 2-4% of GDP on childcare and early education. The US national average is about 0.15%, with most states below 0.3%. Even the highest-spending US states don't come close to the Nordic floor.

Wage ratios tell a different story. In every country, childcare workers earn less than the median worker. But the gap varies. In the US, childcare workers in some states earn barely half the median wage. In the Nordics and Canada, the ratio is closer to 70-75%. Higher wages in Nordic countries reflect unionization, credential requirements, and public-sector employment.

GDP per child varies widely within the US, from about $0.8M per child under 5 in Mississippi to over $3M in some northeastern states. This reflects overall economic capacity, not childcare policy, but it provides context for how much room states have to invest.

How countries organize childcare
Nordic countries

The Nordic countries treat childcare as public infrastructure, much the way most countries treat primary school. Sweden's system is the most well-known example. Nearly all children ages 1 to 5 are enrolled in publicly funded preschool (86% overall, rising to about 95% for ages 3 to 5). Parents pay fees capped at 3% of gross household income, which works out to roughly $190 per month at the cap. Childcare workers are public employees with standardized credentials and training requirements. The result is a system that is close to universal in practice, not just in theory.

Denmark, Norway, and Finland follow similar models with their own variations. Denmark enrolls about 95% of children ages 3 to 5 and backs that up with some of the highest public investment in the world: 2.29% of GDP, the largest share among the countries in this report. Norway goes a step further by guaranteeing a legal right to a childcare place from age 1, and enrollment for ages 3 to 5 sits at 97%. Finland takes a unique approach by granting every child a universal right to early childhood education and care from birth, while also offering families a home care allowance as an alternative. Finland's enrollment for ages 3 to 5 is about 89%, somewhat lower than its neighbors, likely reflecting the home care option. Across all four Nordic countries, public spending on childcare ranges from 1.64% to 2.29% of GDP, and childcare workers earn between 70% and 87% of the national median wage.

Canada

Canada is in the middle of a significant transition. For decades, the country's childcare system looked more American than Nordic: a mix of private providers, long waitlists, and high fees, with one major exception. Quebec launched a low-fee childcare program in 1997 that now charges $9.35 per day, and the province's system has become a proof of concept for what public investment in childcare can accomplish. In 2021, the federal government announced the Canada-Wide Early Learning and Child Care system, with the goal of reaching $10-a-day fees nationwide by 2026. That rollout is still underway, and progress varies by province. Nationally, about 31% of children ages 0 to 12 have access to a regulated childcare space, and Canada spends roughly 0.44% of GDP on childcare. That puts it well above the United States but still far below Nordic levels.

United States

The United States has no federal universal childcare program. What exists instead is a patchwork: state-funded pre-K programs, the federally funded Head Start program, a child care tax credit, and a subsidy system for lower-income families that reaches only a fraction of those eligible. Taken together, state pre-K and Head Start cover about 28% of children ages 3 to 4 nationally. Public spending on childcare amounts to roughly 0.15% of GDP, an order of magnitude below the Nordic average.

The national average, though, masks enormous variation across states. Washington, D.C. enrolls 81% of 3- and 4-year-olds in public pre-K programs, and Vermont reaches 71%. These are numbers that would not look out of place in Scandinavia. But several states have virtually no public pre-K at all: New Hampshire enrolls about 3.5% of that age group, and Idaho and Wyoming are similarly low. Whether a child has access to affordable, publicly funded early education depends almost entirely on which state they live in.

The workforce side of the picture reflects these structural differences. In Nordic countries, childcare work is a recognized profession with wages that reflect it. In the United States, the median childcare worker earns about $32,000 per year, roughly 62% of the overall median wage. In some states, that ratio drops below 50%. Low wages drive high turnover, which undermines the stability and quality of care. It is difficult to build a high-quality system on a workforce that cannot afford to stay in it.

The core question these comparisons raise is not really about childcare preferences or parenting philosophies. It is about whether a country's policy framework treats early childhood education as a public good, the way it treats roads or schools or clean water, or as a private expense that families are mostly expected to figure out on their own. The Nordic countries and, increasingly, Canada have chosen the first path. The United States, at the federal level, has not, though a handful of states are building something that looks quite different from the national norm.

Two models of childcare

The difference between the US and Nordic approaches isn't just about funding levels. It's about whether childcare is treated as a universal public service or as a private consumption good.

In Sweden, Denmark, Norway, and Finland, childcare functions like public schooling. Enrollment rates for ages 3-5 reach 80-95%. Parents have a legal right to a slot. Workers are typically public employees with standardized credentials. The state covers most of the cost, with parental fees capped at a small share of income.

In the United States, childcare is primarily a market transaction. Families piece together a mix of center-based care, home-based care, grandparent help, and informal arrangements. Public support comes through a patchwork of programs: Head Start, the Child Care Development Fund, state pre-K, and tax credits. The result is that parents pay a lot while workers earn very little. Childcare workers in the US are among the lowest-paid occupations, ranking 10th from the bottom out of more than 800 tracked by the Bureau of Labor Statistics.

Canada is in the middle of a transition. In 2021, the federal government launched the Canada-Wide Early Learning and Child Care system, aiming for $10-a-day fees by 2026. Quebec has operated a low-fee universal system since 1997, and its enrollment rates and maternal labor force participation both exceed the rest of Canada. But nationally, only about 31% of children aged 0-12 have access to a regulated childcare space, with huge variation by province.

The wage-quality problem

Childcare is one of the most labor-intensive sectors in any economy. Staff-to-child ratios mean that labor costs make up the vast majority of operating expenses. This creates a basic tension: raising worker pay directly raises the cost of care.

In the US, this tension gets resolved by keeping wages low. The median childcare worker earns about $32,000 a year, roughly 62% of the overall median wage. Turnover is high. Many workers qualify for public assistance.

Nordic countries resolve the tension differently. The state absorbs the cost of higher wages through public funding, so parents don't face the full price pass-through. Workers earn closer to the national median, turnover is lower, and credential requirements are higher. Research suggests that when US states raise minimum wages, childcare prices rise 4-8% for every 10% wage increase, a much larger pass-through than in most service industries, because labor's share of total costs is so high.

Canada's wage picture is mixed. The national wage ratio is about 75%, higher than the US but achieved partly through a broader occupational definition that includes some education workers alongside childcare workers.

The invisible economy of care

GDP only counts market transactions. When a parent leaves paid work to care for a child at home, GDP falls, even though the total amount of childcare in the economy hasn't changed. This means countries with formalized childcare systems look richer in national accounts, while countries where care happens at home look less productive.

Nordic countries reduce this measurement distortion by moving care into the formal sector. They also run detailed time-use surveys and publish satellite accounts that estimate the value of unpaid household work. The US runs the American Time Use Survey, which shows that unpaid childcare is enormous in economic terms, but this data rarely enters mainstream policy debates.

One way to estimate the size of the invisible care economy: take the per-child cost of formal care, multiply it by the number of children not enrolled. By this measure, the implicit value of informal care in the US runs into the hundreds of billions of dollars annually. Nordic countries have a much smaller informal sector because most children are in publicly funded care.

What's working in the US

The US average tells a story of underinvestment, but averages hide a lot. Several states and cities have built systems that look dramatically different from the national picture. These three examples show what becomes possible when public policy treats childcare as infrastructure rather than a private expense.

New York City: Scale and ambition

New York City is the closest thing the US has to a Nordic-style universal system. Since launching Universal Pre-K for all four-year-olds in 2014, the city has enrolled roughly 62% of its young children in publicly funded programs, more than double the national rate of 28.3%. Total public spending on early childhood exceeds $3.2 billion, about 0.25% of the city's GDP. That is still well below Nordic levels of 1.6% to 2.3%, but it is nearly twice the national average of 0.15%.

The city has since expanded to 3-K for All, pushing universal access down to three-year-olds. What makes New York's approach distinctive is not just its scale but its structure. The system blends DOE-run classrooms with community-based organizations and Head Start providers, creating a mixed-delivery network that meets families where they are. The city has also pushed for wage parity between community-based early childhood teachers and their public school counterparts, recognizing that a system built on poverty wages for its workforce is not really a system at all.

The challenges are real. Expanding access without enough qualified teachers creates staffing bottlenecks. Wage parity remains a goal, not yet a reality for most providers. And $3.2 billion is a lot of money for a city budget to absorb, which raises questions about long-term fiscal sustainability without state or federal support. But as a proof of concept for what an American city can build when it decides to invest, New York stands alone.

New Mexico: Oil revenue becomes childcare funding

New Mexico took an unusual path. In 2022, voters approved a constitutional amendment directing a portion of the state's oil and gas revenue into a dedicated early childhood trust fund. In September 2025, New Mexico became the first US state to launch universal childcare, meaning any family in the state can access subsidized care regardless of income. The state also created a cabinet-level Early Childhood Education and Care Department, giving the issue institutional weight that it lacks in most state governments.

New Mexico's enrollment rate of 43% is still well below the Nordic range of 89% to 97%, but the universal framework is new and access is still ramping up. What makes this example important is the funding mechanism. Most US childcare programs depend on annual appropriations, which makes them vulnerable to budget cycles and political shifts. By tying funding to a constitutionally protected trust, New Mexico insulated its system from the year-to-year fights that have stalled progress in other states. It is an approach that turns a finite natural resource into long-term human capital investment.

The open question is whether supply can keep up with the new demand. Rural New Mexico, like rural areas everywhere, struggles with provider shortages. Building the physical and workforce infrastructure to match the policy ambition will take years. But the structural foundation is in place in a way that no other state has matched.

Vermont: Quiet consistency

Vermont does not generate the same headlines as New Mexico or New York, but its numbers tell a striking story. At 71% public enrollment for three- and four-year-olds, Vermont is closer to Finland than to the US average. The state has offered universal pre-K since 2015 through Act 166, which provides 10 hours per week of publicly funded education for all children ages three to five, delivered through a mixed system of public schools, private centers, and home-based providers.

The mixed-delivery model is key. Rather than building a parallel public system from scratch, Vermont funds slots wherever they exist, which has allowed the state to reach high enrollment without the massive capital expenditure that a school-based-only approach would require. The state has recently begun expanding access to younger children, pushing toward something closer to a birth-to-five continuum.

Vermont's limitations are also instructive. Ten hours per week is meaningful for early learning but does not solve the full childcare problem for working parents who need 40 or 50 hours of coverage. The gap between a pre-K program and a childcare system is real, and Vermont's enrollment numbers partly reflect the narrower scope of what is being measured. Still, Vermont demonstrates that sustained, incremental policy can produce outcomes that look nothing like the US average, even without a dramatic single moment of reform.

Data and methodology

US states: Government spending combines federal program data (Head Start, CCDF, TANF childcare, IDEA, MIECHV, SSBG, Title I) with state pre-K spending from NIEER, expressed as a share of state GDP from BEA. Wage data comes from the BLS Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics (OEWS) survey for 2024. Enrollment rates are state pre-K plus Head Start enrollment (ages 3-4) as a share of the age 3-4 population (ACS 2023 5-year estimates). The spending figure is a partial stack and does not capture all childcare-related expenditures.

Canada: Enrollment data comes from the Survey on Early Learning and Child Care Arrangements (SELCCA) via Statistics Canada, covering ages 4-5. Spending and wage data are available at the national level from OECD and Statistics Canada (NOC 422 occupation group). Province-level spending and wage breakdowns are not yet available in this report.

Nordic countries: Spending data comes from the OECD Social Expenditure Database (SOCX) and national statistical agencies. Wage data comes from national statistics offices (Statistics Denmark LONS20, Statistics Finland, Statistics Norway, Statistics Sweden). Enrollment data comes from OECD UOE data (ISCED 0, ages 3-5).

Enrollment comparability: Age bands differ slightly across countries: the US covers ages 3-4, Canada covers ages 4-5, and the Nordics cover ages 3-5. The US figure counts only publicly funded program slots (state pre-K and Head Start), while the Nordic and Canadian figures include all formal early childhood education and care. Enrollment rates measure headcounts and do not capture intensity (part-time vs. full-time attendance).

Other comparability notes: Occupation definitions differ (US SOC, Nordic ISCO/national codes, Canada NOC). School starting ages vary, affecting what counts as "childcare" vs "education." Spending figures for the US are proxy estimates that likely undercount total public investment. All values are converted to comparable terms (shares of GDP, wage ratios) rather than nominal amounts to reduce currency and price-level distortions.
